A safety standard for open AI

Open-weight models are everywhere — but there's no common yardstick for how safe they are. We're building one.

The Open LLM Safety Index is an independent effort to test the safety of every open-weight large language model and publish the results as a single, easy-to-read score from 1 to 5.

Why this exists

You can download thousands of open-weight AI models today and drop them straight into a product, an app, or an internal tool. That openness is genuinely good — it drives research, competition, and independence from a handful of big providers.

But it comes with a blind spot. When you pick a model, you can compare its speed, its size, and how well it writes code or answers trivia. What you can't easily find out is how safely it behaves — how hard it is to trick into producing dangerous content, and how reliably its guardrails hold up under pressure. That information exists in scattered research papers, but not in a form a normal person choosing a model can actually use.

Our goal: test the safety of every open-weight model, so the people building with them are informed — and so there's a shared standard for what "safe enough" means.

What the score means

Each model gets a safety score from 1 (Fragile) to 5 (Robust). A high score means the model held its guardrails even when we actively tried to break them; a low score means it gave in easily. One number, the same way for every model, so you can compare at a glance — with the full evidence one click away.

Right now the score reflects jailbreak robustness: resistance to prompts crafted to bypass a model's safety training. That's the first dimension we measure, and it's the whole of the score today. It won't be the last — safety has more than one facet, and the index is built to grow into a broader, combined standard over time.

Two principles

Independent

We don't build these models and we're not paid by anyone who does. The index is a neutral third party — the same tests, applied the same way, to everyone.

Fully transparent

Every score is reproducible. The benchmarks are public, the code is open source, and we publish the complete transcript behind every result — every prompt we sent, every raw response the model gave, and every judgement. Nothing is hidden behind an aggregate number. If you don't trust the score, read the evidence and check it yourself.

A note on responsible testing

To measure whether a model resists misuse, we have to actually try to misuse it — using prompts from established, published safety benchmarks. This is standard practice in AI safety research. The stored transcripts contain real, uncensored model outputs, some of which attempt to comply with harmful requests; they exist so the results can be audited, not as a how-to. We treat them as the red-teaming research artifacts they are.
